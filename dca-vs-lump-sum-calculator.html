What is DCA vs Lump Sum investing?

Dollar cost averaging, or DCA, means investing a fixed amount at regular intervals instead of investing everything at once. Lump sum investing means putting the full amount into the market immediately. This calculator compares both approaches using the same total amount so you can see how they behave over time.

In a rising market, lump sum investing often has the advantage because the money starts compounding earlier. In choppy or declining markets, DCA can feel safer because it spreads entry points across time and reduces timing risk. This calculator helps you compare the tradeoff instead of guessing.

How this calculator works

The calculator assumes that both strategies use the same total capital. Lump sum invests all of it at the beginning. DCA divides the capital evenly across the chosen DCA period and invests it monthly. After the DCA period ends, the invested balance continues compounding until the end of the analysis horizon.

Monthly return = (1 + annual return)^(1 / 12) - 1
Lump sum = full amount invested at month 0
DCA = total amount / DCA months, invested monthly
Final value = invested balance + compound growth over time

The chart shows both strategies month by month so you can see whether lump sum pulls ahead early or DCA catches up later. This is especially useful for long-term investors who want a simple answer without doing the math by hand.

Why this comparison matters

This comparison matters because the biggest mistake in investing is not usually the asset itself, but the entry timing. Some investors want to reduce regret and emotional pressure with DCA. Others want to maximize time in the market with lump sum investing. Both can be valid, depending on your goals and risk tolerance.
